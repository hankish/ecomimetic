# Manual

<p class="lead">The Ecomimetic Design Manual is a living document. What follows is an early draft — structured enough to be useful, open enough to keep evolving.</p>

## How to Use This Manual

This manual is not a recipe book. It does not tell you what to build or how to build it. Instead, it offers a set of principles, frameworks, and heuristics for developing your own judgment about how to design well within living systems.

Read it linearly the first time. Return to individual sections as specific questions arise in your practice. The manual is most useful when held alongside a real project — as a thinking partner, not an authority.

## Part One: Foundations

### 1.1 Ecology as Context, Not Backdrop

Every designed object or system exists within an ecological context that it will inevitably affect. The question is not whether this impact occurs, but whether it is legible, intentional, and reciprocal. Ecomimetic design begins with the practice of making this relationship visible before making any formal decisions.

### 1.2 The Three Timescales

Ecomimetic design operates simultaneously across three timescales: the project timescale (months to a few years), the generation timescale (decades), and the ecological timescale (centuries to millennia). Most design practice optimizes only for the first. This manual asks you to hold all three.

### 2.2 Design for Succession

Ecological communities change over time through processes of succession — early colonizers give way to later species, complexity accumulates, resilience deepens. Ecomimetic design treats this succession as a feature, not a problem to be managed. Materials, structures, and programs are chosen for how they will participate in succession, not how they will resist it.

## Part Three: Practice

### 3.1 Site Reading

Before designing, read the site — not just its topography and climate data, but its ecological history, its current species assemblages, its hydrological behavior, its relationship to surrounding landscapes. This reading takes time and requires collaboration with ecologists, local historians, and the people who know the land.
