## All the Money in the World

Imagine that you had 50 trillion US dollars in a bank account and a mission to use it to solve some major civilisational problem. Let's pick something relatively straightforward like removing plastic from all of earth's waterways. 

In order to accomplish this task you're going to need to create an organization of some sort. Maybe it's a non-profit, maybe a foundation, maybe an investment firm, but an organization of some sort will need to exist. And it'll be truly massive in size and scope. It might eventually involve millions of people. And it'll need to solve tremendous challenges: scientific, logistical, economic, political, and more.

Let's think through some scenarios:
* Imagine that you have to be the chief executive yourself. Do you think the org will be successful? Why or why not? 
* Imagine that the chief executive is the former head of a major Wall Street bank. Now do you think it'll be successful? Why or why not? 
* Imagine that the chief executive is the greatest and most well-suited leader conceivable. Will it succeed? Why or why not?

If we're really honest with ourselves, it can be extremely difficult to imagine that such a gargantuan effort could succeed without getting lost or becoming corrupted in some way, even with a fairly well-defined problem and even if money were no object. That's because it's not truly a problem of resources. Rather it's a problem of organizational coordination and trust at massive scale.

And deep down most of us have an intuitive understanding of a fundamental truth about organizations: The bigger an organization gets, the less effective it becomes.

Larger organizations may be able to marshall greater resources, but they become less efficient in critical ways which limit their ability to solve large, complex problems. That's why we have incredibly few examples of successful large scale human coordination around complex problems, particularly when the goals are unclear or when we're going up against entrenched interests.

This intuitive truth is so innate that it can feel inevitable--like gravity or entropy--a fundamental limiter of the possibilities of human collaboration. From multinational corporations to sprawling government bureaucracies, we're surrounded with uncountable examples of organizational failure, corruption, and incompetence.

But the failure of organizations to successfully scale is not inevitable. It is merely an inevitable consequence of structuring them as giant pyramids. **Let's call it the Pyramid Problem.**

## The Pyramid Problem

As used here, the Pyramid Problem describes the paradoxical nature of hierarchical organizational structures. As they grow larger, command more resources, and involve more humans, we would hope that they would generally get better at tackling ever bigger problems. On the contrary, while larger bureaucratic structures are indeed able to operate at greater scale and drive greater overall impact, they simultaneously become less effective in certain key ways. 

Large hierarchical organizations grow dumber, slower, less creative, and ultimately less "human" as they scale, ultimately leading them to become less reliable and less trusted. These adverse effects tend to increase as hierarchical organizations grow, eventually leading to a state of "hierarchical coordination collapse", imposing a theoretical limit on the maximum scale of a hierarchical organization.

In order to underscore the need for an alternative approach while also framing the solution space, let's explore the five fatal flaws of huge hierarchies.

### In Defense of Hierarchies

Before we get hyper-critical about hierarchies, let's qualify our critique with a truth: Hierarchies aren't intrinsically bad. In fact they're an incredibly effective way to organize humans, up to a certain operational scale.

To go further, we should distinguish between hierarchies as a fundamental concept (one that is a natural consequences of the asymmetric distribution of specialized skills and domain expertise) versus hierarchical organizational structures (aka bureaucracies). There are approaches to architecting the coordination of specialists and non-specialists that look nothing like traditional bureaucracy. In fact, those approaches are a key part of ecomimetic org structures.

So in defense of hierarchies: At smaller scales of coordination or in situations when operational reliability is the principal consideration (emergency rooms, fire departments, etc), hierarchical structures are the obvious approach. The goal of this section is not to advocate against hierarchy as an organizational tool, but rather to clarify the limits of the tool at scale.

### The Five Fatal Flaws of Huge Hierarchies

#### Flaw 1: Information Compression Leads to Low Intelligence

Hierarchies depend on information flowing upward through successive layers of management. At each step the signal must be summarized, simplified, and reframed in order to be digestible by the next level. Nuance is lost. Local context disappears. Subtle signals are filtered out entirely.

By the time information reaches the top of a large pyramid, thousands of observations from the edge of the system have been compressed into a few bullet points on a slide deck. The leaders making decisions are therefore not interacting with reality itself, but with a heavily abstracted narrative about reality.

As hierarchies grow larger, this compression effect intensifies. More people generate more signals, requiring more layers of filtering. The result is that even though the organization possesses enormous collective intelligence, its decisions are increasingly based on drastically simplified representations of the world.

Large pyramids do not lack intelligence, they simply cannot access it.

#### Flaw 2: Cognitive Throughput Leads to Slow Responsiveness

Every hierarchical system ultimately depends on a relatively small number of people to authorize key decisions, but the complexity of a large organization produces far more decisions than any small group of humans can reasonably process.

As scale increases, decision _demand_ rises exponentially while decision _capacity_ grows only marginally. The result is a structural bottleneck: approvals stack up, decisions are delayed, and organizations begin to respond to events long after conditions have already changed.

To cope with this overload, bureaucracies introduce additional procedures, reporting structures, and approval gates--mechanisms which further slow the system.

#### Flaw 3: Control Node Fragility Leads to Stagnation Bias

In a hierarchical structure, authority and responsibility are concentrated at the top. This creates powerful control nodes: Individuals or small groups whose decisions shape the entire system. 

When a small number of people carry the burden of coordination for thousands, the cost of mistakes becomes extremely high. This leads to **control node fragility**, consisting of two different adverse effects:

1. **Control Node Stress:** The increased pressure on control nodes leads to increased chance of burnout, interpersonal stress, and influences the quality of decision-making. There is no upper limit on this effect. The larger the organization grows, the greater the stress.

2. **Control Node Reliance:** Control nodes tend to become highly reliant on specific individuals in order to continue functioning. When high-value individuals burnout, move on, or fail, entire parts of the organization can fail along with them. This brittleness introduces risk that grows along with increasing control node stress.

Under these conditions, **stagnation bias** emerges: The high cost and high probability of losing control nodes means that the needs of certain high-value individuals can become competitive with the needs of the organization as a whole. Leaders become incentivized to avoid disruption, favor predictability, and protect existing structures rather than challenge them.

As pyramids grow larger, these effects increase. Over time this produces a systemic bias toward caution, stability, and inertia—-even when the surrounding environment demands adaptation.

#### Flaw 4: Evolutionary Constraint Leads to Slow Innovation

Innovation is fundamentally an evolutionary process: many ideas are tested, most fail, and a few succeed. The faster a system can run these experiments, the faster it learns, the faster it innovates.

Hierarchies constrain this evolutionary dynamic. Because resources and authority are centralized, most experimentation must be approved before it can begin. Ideas compete for limited attention from decision-makers who may be far removed from the context in which the ideas originated.

As a result, only a small number of experiments are allowed to proceed, and they often reflect the priorities of leadership rather than the insights of those closest to emerging opportunities.

Over time this dramatically reduces the rate at which new approaches can be explored. The organization becomes less capable of discovering novel solutions precisely when its scale demands greater creativity.

#### Flaw 5: Relational Distance Leads to Low Trust & Soullessness

As hierarchies grow taller, relational distance grows in two reinforcing directions.

First, there is **internal relational distance**. Each additional layer of management places more separation between leaders and the people doing the work. Over time, the individuals responsible for shaping strategy and making high-impact decisions become increasingly removed from the lived realities of the organization. Lower-level contributors feel unseen and unheard, while leaders must rely on filtered reports rather than direct experience. Trust within the organization gradually erodes as both sides lose the sense that they are truly participating in the same system.

Second, hierarchies produce **control-node isolation**. As control-node pressure increases, so does the need to protect and manage access to them. Schedules tighten. Gatekeepers emerge. Protocols multiply. Senior decision-makers become surrounded by layers of administrative insulation designed to control information and manage risk.

The result is that the control nodes of the system become socially and logistically isolated—not only from their own organization, but also from customers, stakeholders, and the wider world they are meant to serve.

These two dynamics compound over time. Eventually the organization begins to feel less like a community and more like a machine. The informal bonds of trust that sustain collaboration weaken, and the institution takes on the familiar "soulless" quality we associate with large bureaucracies: a diffuse sense of impersonality and institutional hollowing.

When this happens, the social contracts that hold the organization together—and that legitimize its role within the broader society—begin to fray.


### The Structural Limits of Hierarchy

Taken together, these five flaws reveal a structural paradox at the heart of large hierarchies. The very mechanisms that allow pyramids to scale—layered authority, centralized decision-making, and formalized channels of communication—also create compounding constraints on intelligence, responsiveness, adaptability, resilience, and trust. 

As the pyramid grows, the coordination system becomes increasingly disconnected from the complexity it is meant to manage. More resources, more people, and more activity do not necessarily produce greater collective capability. Instead, they gradually introduce friction, distortion, and fragility into the system itself.

At a certain scale, these effects begin to reinforce one another, pushing the organization toward a state of **hierarchical coordination collapse**—a point where the structure can no longer effectively sense, decide, adapt, or maintain trust at the speed and complexity required by its environment. 

The pyramid problem therefore points to a deeper design challenge: if hierarchies eventually encounter these limits, then large-scale human coordination must ultimately depend on a different type of organizational architecture.


### Why is this important now?

Hierarchical organizations are not new. Human societies have relied on them for thousands of years to coordinate armies, governments, religions, and large economic enterprises. For most of history this worked reasonably well because the systems we were coordinating were smaller, simpler, and slower.

What has changed is the _operating environment_. Four factors in particular have pushed hierarchical coordination closer to its limits:

* **Pace of Change**
  Technological progress, globalization, and rapid cultural shifts have dramatically accelerated the rate at which conditions change. Hierarchies, which rely on layered decision-making and approval chains, struggle to react at the speed modern environments demand.

* **Level of Complexity**
  Modern challenges exist within deeply interconnected systems: economic, technological, ecological, and social. No small group of centralized decision-makers can fully comprehend or manage this level of complexity.

* **Quantity of Information**
  The digital age has produced an explosion of information and real-time signals about what is happening in the world. Hierarchical systems were designed for information scarcity, not abundance, and their filtering structures increasingly suppress the very intelligence the system needs to function.

* **Scale of Coordination**
  Most significantly and most simply, the sheer scale of the human population is outstripping the coordination capabilities of bureaucratic structures.

As these factors show no signs of abating, we must answer the question: How do we effectively organize ourselves without relying solely on hierarchy? 

Or perhaps more accurately, since we know that at a smaller scale hierarchy will continue to exist: How do we architect structures that allow many individuals and smaller hierarchies to coordinate without simply becoming larger hierarchies?
